    Pythagoran Theorem

    This article is taken from IXL. You can view it here.

    The Pythagorean theorem describes a special relationship between the sides of a right triange.

    A right triangle is made up of two legs and a hypotenuse. The legs meet at a 90° angle. The hypotenuse is the side opposite the right angle. The hypotenuse is always the longest side. We use a and b to represent the lengths of the legs, and c to represent the length of the hypotenuse.

    According to the Pythagorean theorem, the square of the hypotenuse is equal to the sum of the squares of the legs.

    a2 + b2 = c2

    How to Calculate the Area of an n-Sided Polygon

    Whether you're working with a perfect hexagon or a complex, irregular shape, here is how to find the area of any polygon.

    1. Regular Polygons

    A polygon is regular if all its sides and interior angles are equal.

    Area = n×s2 4×tan180n

    Common Shortcut Formulas

    • Equilateral Triangle: Area = (√3 / 4) × s²
    • Square: Area = s²
    • Regular Hexagon: Area = (3√3 / 2) × s²

    2. Using the Apothem

    If you know the apothem (a)—the distance from the center to the midpoint of any side—the formula becomes much simpler:

    Area = ½ × Perimeter × Apothem

    3. Irregular Polygons (Shoelace Formula)

    If the sides are of different lengths, the most accurate method is the Shoelace Formula.

    Volume of an n‑Sided Regular Polyhedron

    How to compute the volume of 3D solids built from a regular n‑sided polygon.

    1. Area of a Regular n‑Sided Polygon

    Any regular polygon with n sides of length s has area:

    Area = n×s2 4×tanπn

    This formula comes from dividing the polygon into n congruent isosceles triangles.

    2. Volume of a Prism with an n‑Sided Base

    A prism with height h and a regular n‑gon base has volume:

    V= Area×h

    Substituting the polygon area:

    V= n×s2×h 4×tanπn

    3. Volume of a Pyramid with an n‑Sided Base

    A pyramid with the same base and height h has volume:

    V= 1 3 × Area × h

    This works for any regular n‑gon base.

    4. What About “Regular Polyhedra”?

    In strict geometry, only five Platonic solids are truly “regular polyhedra.” They correspond to specific values of n:

    • n = 3 → Tetrahedron, Octahedron, Icosahedron
    • n = 4 → Cube
    • n = 5 → Dodecahedron

    For general n, the most natural 3D solids are prisms and pyramids built from a regular n‑gon base — and their volumes always come from:

    Volume = (Area of base) × (height)

    Surface Area of an n‑Sided Regular Polyhedron

    How to compute the surface area of 3D solids built from a regular n‑sided polygon.

    1. Perimeter and Area of a Regular n‑Sided Polygon

    A regular polygon with n sides of length s has:

    • Perimeter: P = n × s
    • Area:
    Area = n×s2 4×tanπn

    This polygon will be the base of the 3D solid.

    2. Surface Area of a Prism with an n‑Sided Base

    A prism has two identical n‑sided bases and n rectangular faces.

    Total Surface Area:

    SA = 2×Area + Perimeter×h

    The term Perimeter × h represents the combined area of all the rectangular side faces.

    3. Surface Area of a Pyramid with an n‑Sided Base

    A pyramid has one n‑sided base and n triangular faces.

    Total Surface Area:

    SA = Area + Lateral Area

    Each triangular face has area:

    s×ℓ 2

    where ℓ is the slant height. Since there are n triangles:

    Lateral Area = n×s×ℓ 2

    4. Surface Area and “Regular Polyhedra”

    Only five solids are truly regular polyhedra (the Platonic solids), and each has its own closed‑form surface area formula.

    • Tetrahedron (triangular faces)
    • Cube (square faces)
    • Octahedron (triangular faces)
    • Dodecahedron (pentagonal faces)
    • Icosahedron (triangular faces)

    For general n, prisms and pyramids built from a regular n‑gon base are the most natural solids, and their surface areas always come from:

    Surface Area = Base Area + Lateral Area

    Surface Area of the Platonic Solids

    Formulas for the surface area of all five Platonic solids in terms of edge length.

    1. What Are Platonic Solids?

    A Platonic solid is a 3D shape where all faces are congruent regular polygons, and the same number of faces meet at each vertex. There are exactly five:

    • Tetrahedron
    • Cube
    • Octahedron
    • Dodecahedron
    • Icosahedron

    Let the edge length of each solid be a.

    2. Tetrahedron

    A regular tetrahedron has 4 equilateral triangle faces.

    Surface Area:

    SA= 4 × 3×a2 4 = 3×a2

    3. Cube

    A cube has 6 square faces.

    Surface Area:

    SA= 6×a2

    4. Octahedron

    A regular octahedron has 8 equilateral triangle faces.

    Surface Area:

    SA= 8 × 3×a2 4 = 2×3×a2

    5. Dodecahedron

    A regular dodecahedron has 12 regular pentagon faces.

    Surface Area:

    SA= 3 × 25+10×5 × a2

    6. Icosahedron

    A regular icosahedron has 20 equilateral triangle faces.

    Surface Area:

    SA= 20 × 3×a2 4 = 5×3×a2

    Cube 6 Square 6a²
    Octahedron 8 Equilateral triangle 2√3 · a²
    Dodecahedron 12 Pentagon 3√(25 + 10√5) · a²
    Icosahedron 20 Equilateral triangle 5√3 · a²

    Surface Area Cheat Sheet for Common Solids

    Quick formulas for the surface area of spheres, cylinders, cones, prisms, and more.

    1. Sphere

    Surface Area:

    SA= 4πr2

    Where r is the radius.

    2. Cylinder

    Surface Area:

    SA= 2πr(r+h)

    This includes the top, bottom, and curved surface. r = radius, h = height.

    3. Cone

    Surface Area:

    SA= πr(r+ℓ)

    ℓ is the slant height. Formula includes the circular base and the curved surface.

    4. Rectangular Prism (Box)

    Surface Area:

    SA= 2( lw+ lh+ wh )

    Where l = length, w = width, h = height.

    5. Triangular Prism

    Surface Area:

    SA = 2 × (area of triangle) + (perimeter of triangle × height of prism)

    Works for any triangular base.

    6. Pyramid (Any Base)

    Surface Area:

    SA= Base Area + Lateral Area

    Lateral area is the sum of the triangular faces. For a regular pyramid:

    Lateral Area = P×ℓ 2

    P = perimeter of base, ℓ = slant height.

    7. Cube (Special Case)

    Surface Area:

    SA= 6a2

    Where a is the edge length.

    How to Write an Essay

    A clear guide to the structure of a standard academic essay.

    1. Introduction Paragraph

    The introduction sets the stage for your essay. It should grab the reader’s attention, introduce the topic, and end with a strong thesis statement.

    • Hook: An interesting fact, question, or statement.
    • Background: Brief context the reader needs.
    • Thesis Statement: Your main argument or claim.

    Example thesis: “Regular exercise improves mental health, boosts energy, and strengthens the body.”

    2. Body Paragraphs

    Each body paragraph should focus on one main idea that supports your thesis. A typical essay has 2–4 body paragraphs.

    Structure of a Body Paragraph

    • Topic Sentence: Introduces the paragraph’s main idea.
    • Evidence: Facts, quotes, examples, or data.
    • Explanation: How the evidence supports your point.
    • Closing Sentence: Wraps up the idea and transitions to the next paragraph.

    Tip: Use transition words like “Furthermore,” “In addition,” or “However.”

    3. Conclusion Paragraph

    The conclusion restates your thesis in a new way and summarizes your main points. It should leave the reader with a final thought.

    • Restate the Thesis: Say it differently, not word-for-word.
    • Summarize Key Points: Briefly revisit your main ideas.
    • Final Insight: A closing thought, call to action, or reflection.

    Example final insight: “By making small daily choices, anyone can build a healthier lifestyle.”

    4. Essay Structure Overview

    Section Purpose Key Elements
    Introduction Introduce topic and thesis Hook, background, thesis
    Body Paragraph 1 Main supporting idea Topic sentence, evidence, explanation
    Body Paragraph 2 Second supporting idea Topic sentence, evidence, explanation
    Body Paragraph 3 Optional third idea Topic sentence, evidence, explanation
    Conclusion Wrap up and reflect Restated thesis, summary, final insight

    5. Quick Tips for Strong Essays

    • Stay on topic — every sentence should support your thesis.
    • Use formal, clear language.
    • Vary sentence structure to keep writing interesting.
    • Proofread for grammar, punctuation, and clarity.
    • Use transitions to connect ideas smoothly.

    Persuasive Essay Format

    A guide to writing a clear, convincing persuasive essay.

    1. Introduction

    The introduction sets up your argument and prepares the reader for your position. A strong persuasive introduction includes:

    • Hook: A surprising fact, bold statement, or question.
    • Background: Brief context about the issue.
    • Claim (Thesis): Your clear position on the topic.

    Example claim: “Schools should start later in the morning to improve student health and performance.”

    2. Body Paragraphs

    Each body paragraph should support your claim with strong reasoning and evidence. Most persuasive essays include 2–4 body paragraphs.

    Structure of a Persuasive Body Paragraph

    • Reason: A main point that supports your claim.
    • Evidence: Facts, statistics, examples, or expert opinions.
    • Explanation: How the evidence proves your point.
    • Counterargument (optional): Acknowledge the other side.
    • Rebuttal: Explain why your side is stronger.

    Tip: Persuasive writing is strongest when you show you understand both sides.

    3. Conclusion

    The conclusion reinforces your argument and leaves the reader thinking. A persuasive conclusion should:

    • Restate your claim in a fresh way.
    • Summarize your strongest reasons.
    • Call to action: Tell the reader what should happen next.

    Example call to action: “If schools want healthier, more focused students, they must adopt later start times.”

    4. Persuasive Essay Structure Overview

    Section Purpose Key Elements
    Introduction Present the issue and your position Hook, background, claim
    Body Paragraph 1 Main supporting reason Reason, evidence, explanation
    Body Paragraph 2 Second supporting reason Reason, evidence, explanation
    Body Paragraph 3 Counterargument + rebuttal Opposing view, rebuttal
    Conclusion Reinforce your argument Restated claim, summary, call to action

    5. Quick Tips for Persuasive Writing

    • Use strong, confident language.
    • Choose evidence that is specific and credible.
    • Avoid emotional exaggeration—logic is more convincing.
    • Address the other side to strengthen your argument.
    • End with a powerful final message.
